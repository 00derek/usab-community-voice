## Issue
The current junior selection event format relies heavily on single-elimination outcomes, which can make advancement sensitive to draw path and limited match sample size. For an event used to determine national representation, the format should produce results that are as robust and repeatable as possible.

**Tags**: `selection` `tournament-format` `junior` `high-performance` `governance`

## Context
Selection for national representation is one of the highest-stakes outcomes in the domestic junior system. In that setting, format design matters because the event is not only identifying a tournament winner, but also helping determine which athletes best represent current competitive strength.

The current concern is that a single-elimination format can produce outcomes that are overly sensitive to bracket path:

- A strong player who draws another strong player early may be eliminated before there is enough match data to distinguish between draw difficulty and underlying player quality
- A weaker player with a favorable path may advance further than their overall level would suggest
- The event produces relatively few matches per athlete, which limits the ability to evaluate consistency across multiple opponents

This does not mean single elimination is inherently wrong. It is efficient, easy to run, and familiar to participants. The question is whether it is the right format for a selection event where accuracy of outcome may matter more than operational simplicity.

Any format change also creates tradeoffs. Reintroducing a field cap with round-robin initial rounds may reduce draw dependence at the event itself, but it raises an upstream fairness question: if rankings become the qualification gate for a capped event, players may need to attend more sanctioned events to earn entry, increasing travel burden and amplifying regional access differences. A format change should therefore be evaluated together with the qualification pathway.

## Potential Options

> The options below are illustrative examples only and are not necessarily the best or final solutions. Better options may come from the community, clubs, or USAB directly.

- **Option A — Reintroduce a capped field with round-robin initial rounds at JSE**: Cap JSE entries using current rankings or a qualification threshold and run a round-robin group stage before transitioning to knockout. This gives each athlete multiple matches against varied opponents and reduces the influence of any single early-round result.

- **Option B — Keep open entry but redesign the bracket format**: Preserve broad access while exploring alternative formats that create more than one meaningful match for each entrant, such as group play into knockout or enhanced placement pathways. This may preserve access better than a strict cap, but requires more courts and more scheduling complexity.

- **Option C — Two-stage regional pre-qualifier feeding into a national selection event**: Hold regional selection events first, each producing a defined slate of qualifiers for a capped national stage. This reduces national field size organically, distributes travel more geographically, and may create a more balanced path into the final selection event.

- **Option D — Maintain the current format but strengthen seeding and review procedures**: If USAB keeps single elimination, it could still reduce volatility by improving seeding inputs, publishing clearer procedures, and reviewing whether early-round outcomes are systematically distorting final selection results.
