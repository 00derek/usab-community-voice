## Issue
The financial model underpinning USAB-sanctioned badminton is increasingly unsustainable across multiple stakeholder groups simultaneously: families face rising membership costs without visible service improvements, host clubs absorb a 30% USAB revenue cut with JSE hosting further requiring a ~$20,000 bidding commitment that reduces the club's share of revenue on top of growing operational expenses that are especially acute in high-cost regions like NorCal, and umpires receive a $40/day stipend that does not cover their actual cost burden. Without a visible corporate sponsorship strategy or revenue diversification plan, these pressures will compound — threatening the club hosting pipeline, the officiating pipeline, and broad community participation.

## Context
The financial strain cuts across three distinct groups, each experiencing a different dimension of the same structural problem:

**Player and family membership costs**
Annual USAB membership fees, Safe Sport certification requirements, and event entry fees represent a meaningful and growing financial commitment for families — particularly those with multiple junior players. The community's frustration is compounded by a perceived lack of corresponding improvement in services: rankings are delayed, communication is unresponsive, medical coverage is absent at major events, and schedules are announced late. When costs rise without visible service improvements, members naturally ask what value is being delivered in return.

**Host club financial burden**
USAB retains 30% of tournament revenue from sanctioned events. For JSE specifically, the bidding process requires clubs to commit to a ~$20,000 guarantee — not an upfront payment, but a commitment that is deducted from the club's share of revenue, effectively reducing what the host club nets from the event. What remains of the club's share must then cover: venue rental, court setup and equipment, physio (currently an out-of-pocket expense per the medical staffing issue), umpire accommodations and meals, volunteer meals, printing, and administrative overhead. In a high-cost region like NorCal — where venue rental, accommodation, and food costs are significantly above national averages — this margin may become difficult for some clubs to sustain. The $20k revenue commitment may also function as a practical barrier: clubs that cannot absorb the risk of a lower net payout if attendance underperforms may decide not to bid, concentrating JSE hosting among a narrower pool of larger clubs. If hosting becomes financially unattractive, the supply of willing host clubs could contract over time.

**Umpire and official compensation**
Umpires receive a $40/day stipend. Against this, they absorb: travel and commute costs to often-distant venues, long working days (frequently 8–12 hours on court), annual USAB membership fees, and mandatory Safe Sport certification costs. In many cases the net compensation after expenses is negligible or negative. Umpires officiate because they care about the sport — but a compensation model that costs them money is not sustainable as a long-term pipeline strategy. Attrition among certified officials is a foreseeable consequence.

**Revenue diversification gap**
Comparable national sports federations generate meaningful revenue through corporate partnerships — equipment manufacturers, apparel brands, sports medicine providers, nutrition companies, facility operators. If USAB is relying primarily on membership fees and tournament revenue cuts, that creates a zero-sum dynamic in which additional organizational costs are borne largely by the community itself. A stronger corporate sponsorship program could diversify revenue and reduce that pressure.

## Potential Options

> The options below are illustrative examples only and are not necessarily the best or final solutions. Better options may come from the community, clubs, or USAB directly.

- **Option A — Commission a transparent financial sustainability review**: Publish a clear breakdown of how USAB revenue is generated and spent — membership fees, tournament cuts, grants, and any existing sponsorships. Without this baseline, the community cannot assess whether the 30% cut is necessary, whether umpire stipends are constrained by budget or by policy, or where efficiency gains are possible. Transparency here is a prerequisite for any other option.

- **Option B — Revise the host club revenue sharing model**: Review and reduce the 30% tournament cut, or introduce a tiered model where the rate varies by event size, region, or host club financial capacity. Alternatively, define what USAB provides in exchange for the cut (e.g., insurance, software, officiating coordination) so clubs understand the value proposition and USAB is accountable for delivering it.

- **Option C — Establish a structured umpire compensation and expense reimbursement framework**: Increase the daily stipend to a level that reflects actual time and cost commitment, and introduce a transparent expense reimbursement policy for travel above a defined threshold. Separately, subsidize or waive the membership and Safe Sport fees for active certified officials — these are costs of doing USAB's work, not the official's personal obligation.

- **Option D — Develop a corporate sponsorship program**: Identify and pursue partnerships with equipment manufacturers (rackets, shuttles, shoes), sports medicine and physiotherapy providers, sports nutrition brands, and facility operators. Designate a sponsorship lead — staff or volunteer — with a mandate and timeline. Sponsorship revenue can directly offset the costs that are currently passed to clubs, families, and officials.

- **Option E — Create tiered membership with explicit, delivered benefits**: Redesign membership tiers so that higher fees correspond to tangible, specific benefits — priority event registration, ranking visibility tools, coaching resources, or equipment discounts through sponsor partnerships. Members should be able to see exactly what they are paying for, and USAB should be accountable for delivering it.

- **Option F — Establish a regional cost adjustment mechanism for high-cost areas**: Acknowledge that a uniform national financial model disadvantages clubs and players in high cost-of-living regions. Introduce a cost-of-living adjustment for hosting stipends, venue support funds, or reduced revenue cuts for clubs in qualifying regions, funded through sponsorship or a rebalanced national budget.

## Open Questions

- How is the 30% USAB tournament revenue cut allocated internally? Is there a published or accessible budget breakdown showing where this revenue goes?
- What is the fully-loaded cost of hosting a typical ORC or JSE event for a NorCal club, including the ~$20k JSE revenue commitment and all out-of-pocket expenses? What is the realistic net margin after the 30% USAB cut and bidding commitment are applied?
- Has USAB analyzed umpire attrition rates or surveyed active and former officials about financial disincentives?
- What corporate sponsorships, if any, does USAB currently hold, and what revenue do they generate relative to total organizational revenue?
- How do comparable national badminton or racket sport federations (Badminton Canada, Badminton England, USTA) structure their revenue sharing with host clubs and their compensation for officials?
- Is the 30% revenue cut rate uniform across all event types and sizes, or does it vary? When was it last reviewed?
- What is the total annual membership fee burden on a family with two junior players, including all required certifications and event fees?
- Is there a USAB staff role or committee currently responsible for sponsorship and revenue development? If not, who would own this?
