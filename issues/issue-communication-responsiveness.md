## Issue
USAB has no reliable, accountable channel for responding to parent, club, and stakeholder inquiries. Emails go unanswered or receive minimal responses, and critical information reaches some community members informally while others remain unaware — creating a two-tier community where access to information depends on personal connections rather than official channels.

**Tags**: `communication` `responsiveness` `governance` `community` `operations`

## Context
Unresponsiveness is one of the fastest ways to erode community trust. When a parent emails a question about rankings, a rule change, or an event and receives no reply, the practical effect is that the person has no signal that the concern was received, routed, or being addressed. Repeated experiences of this kind can cause community members to disengage, stop raising issues, and lose confidence in the organization.

Two distinct but related problems exist:

**1. Reactive communication — no feedback loop on inquiries**
There is no acknowledged ticketing system, response SLA, or designated point of contact for community inquiries. Parents and clubs have no way of knowing whether their message was received, who is responsible for answering it, or when to expect a response. This is not a resource problem alone — it is a structural absence. Without a system, accountability for responses cannot exist.

**2. Proactive communication — information asymmetry**
Critical information (rule changes, schedule updates, policy decisions) frequently reaches some stakeholders through informal channels — personal relationships with board members, club leadership networks, or word of mouth. While not necessarily creating intentional inequity, it means that the broader community may not be fully aware, or only partially aware, of these changes. The same policy or schedule change that surprises most families was already known to a few. This pattern also amplifies the impact of every other issue— delayed rankings, late schedules, new retirement rules, and venue decisions all generate inquiry volume that goes unaddressed.

## Potential Options

> The options below are illustrative examples only and are not necessarily the best or final solutions. Better options may come from the community, clubs, or USAB directly.

- **Option A — Establish a formal inquiry system with published response SLAs**: Implement a simple ticketing or inquiry form (e.g., a dedicated email alias with auto-acknowledgment, or a lightweight helpdesk tool) with a published commitment — e.g., all inquiries acknowledged within 48 hours, substantive responses within 7 business days. The SLA creates accountability; the acknowledgment alone reduces follow-up volume and signals that messages are received.

- **Option B — Designate a community liaison role**: Assign a specific staff member or volunteer as the official point of contact for community communication — responsible for routing inquiries, ensuring responses, and proactively sharing information. A named, reachable person is more accountable than a generic inbox and builds a consistent relationship between USAB and the community.

- **Option C — Publish a regular official newsletter or announcement digest**: Issue a monthly or bi-monthly newsletter covering upcoming events, recent decisions, rule changes, and answers to frequently asked questions. Proactive publishing reduces repetitive individual inquiries and ensures all community members receive the same information at the same time — removing the information asymmetry that currently advantages those with insider access.

- **Option D — Maintain a living public FAQ and decision log**: Build and actively maintain a publicly accessible FAQ on the USAB website covering the most common topics (ranking process, event eligibility, selection criteria, retirement rules, registration deadlines). Update it in real time when policies change. Reduces inquiry volume by giving families a reliable self-service resource before they need to email.

- **Option E — Establish a community feedback mechanism with visible follow-through**: Create a structured channel — a public issue tracker, a community forum, or a dedicated feedback form — where concerns can be submitted and tracked. Critically, USAB must close the loop publicly: acknowledge receipt, indicate who is reviewing, and publish a resolution or response. Visible follow-through on past concerns is what signals to the community that raising issues is worth doing.
