## Issue
The current ORC format — a 3-day tournament starting from a field as large as round of 128 — results in some players being scheduled 7–9 matches on a single day, creating significant injury risk, extended delays, and poor player experience. Addressing this requires navigating interconnected tradeoffs: ranking point incentives that drive outsized ORC participation, the fairness implications of capping entries, the logistical and financial burden of extending to 4 days, and the downstream costs to host clubs, officials, and volunteers.

**Tags**: `tournament-format` `player-welfare` `ORC` `scheduling` `operations` `ranking` `governance`

## Context
The core problem is a mismatch between field size and available playing time. Starting from round of 128 in a 3-day format means the draw compresses multiple rounds into single days. Players who advance deep — or who enter multiple events — can face a back-to-back-to-back match load that no recovery window can adequately address. A 7–9 match day is not a competitive event; it is an attrition test, and injuries follow accordingly.

This is not simply a scheduling problem — it is a structural one, driven by several reinforcing factors:

**Ranking point incentives**: ORC awards significantly more ranking points than OLC or CRC events. This imbalance creates strong financial and competitive incentives for players to enter ORC regardless of their readiness or match fitness — inflating field sizes beyond what the format can handle. Until this incentive structure is rebalanced, demand for ORC entry will remain disproportionately high.

**Capping creates its own fairness problem**: Simply limiting entries means some players are excluded from a competitive pathway they have a legitimate claim to. A cap without a fair qualification mechanism may entrench advantages for higher-ranked or better-connected players, disadvantaging newer or developing competitors who could otherwise earn their place through open competition.

**Extending to 4 days shifts costs broadly**: A 4-day ORC would require players and families to plan an additional day of absence from school or work. For volunteers, referees, and umpires — many of whom contribute their time across the entire event — an extra day compounds scheduling conflicts and fatigue. Host clubs face a full additional day of suspended normal business operations, plus the incremental cost of venue, officiating, and logistics. These costs are real and must be factored into any solution.

This issue requires structured input from multiple stakeholder groups — players, families, coaches, host clubs, officials, and USAB — before any format change is adopted.

## Potential Options

> The options below are illustrative examples only and are not necessarily the best or final solutions. Better options may come from the community, clubs, or USAB directly.

- **Option A — Cap ORC entries by ranking or qualification threshold**: Limit the ORC draw to a defined field size (e.g., top 64 per event) using current rankings or a qualification event. Reduces per-day match load without extending the tournament. Requires a fair, transparent qualification mechanism and must address the "first cut" problem for players on the bubble.

- **Option B — Extend ORC to a 4-day format**: Spread the draw across an additional day to reduce daily match load to a manageable level. Directly solves the scheduling compression but introduces additional cost and absence burden for players, volunteers, officials, and host clubs that must be explicitly scoped and budgeted before adoption.

- **Option C — Split ORC into two tiered divisions (Open / Development)**: Run a capped Open division for higher-ranked players and a Development division for the remaining field, held concurrently or on adjacent weekends. Reduces per-event field size, preserves access for more players, and could better align ranking point awards to division level.

- **Option D — Rebalance ranking points across ORC, OLC, and CRC**: Reduce the points differential between event tiers to lower the structural incentive that drives excessive ORC participation. If OLC and CRC offer meaningfully competitive point opportunities, field sizes at ORC naturally self-regulate. This is a prerequisite fix regardless of which format option is chosen.

- **Option E — Restructure the draw format to reduce single-day match density**: Replace early-round straight knockout with a round-robin group stage, reducing the number of matches that must be played in rapid succession while maintaining the same number of participants. Requires more courts and scheduling complexity but avoids the entry cap or extra-day tradeoffs.

## Open Questions

- What is the actual maximum number of matches players have been scheduled in a single day at recent ORC events, and has USAB tracked associated injury or retirement rates?
- Why is the current ranking points differential between ORC, OLC, and CRC across event categories? Has this differential been reviewed recently?
- What would a 4-day ORC realistically cost a host club — in venue, officiating, volunteer, and lost business terms — compared to the current 3-day format?
- Are existing ORC host venues contractually or logistically able to accommodate a 4-day event, or would this require different venue selection criteria?
- What qualification or capping criteria would the community consider fair — ranking cutoff, regional quota, qualification event, or open lottery?
- Has USAB modeled what field sizes at ORC would look like if ranking point incentives were rebalanced with OLC/CRC?
- Which stakeholder groups — host clubs, officials associations, player representatives, regional directors — need to be formally consulted before any format change is adopted, and is there a process for doing so?
- Could a phased approach work — e.g., points rebalancing first to reduce demand organically, followed by format review once new participation levels are observed?


## Community Proposal — May 1, 2026

A community-drafted design proposal addresses this issue jointly with the related ranking-points issue (`issue-junior-ranking-points-and-seeding.md`). Key recommendations on the format side:

- **Cap A-flight at R64 for the five crunch events** — Boys' Singles U13/U15/U17 and Mixed Doubles U13/U15. These are the events that historically exceed 64 entries and force R128 brackets into 3-day weekends. Other events stay unchanged.
- **A-flight entry is purely top 64 by national ranking.** No reserved spots, no committee-discretion lane.
- **Run a parallel B-flight** — also R64, for registrants who don't make A-flight by national ranking. Same venue, same Saturday-Monday weekend, hard registration cap of 128 per event. B-flight uses reduced officiating standard (roving umpire / self-reporting) and pays 30% of A-flight at every position.
- **No fourth day required.** All A-flight and B-flight play happens Saturday-Monday. Friday is not used for on-court activity. A Friday qualifier was considered and rejected as a de facto 4th-day extension that conflicts with the school/work absence and host-cost concerns originally raised.
- **Edge cases (BWF-experienced, playing-up) are not addressed in this proposal.** A wildcard mechanism was considered and rejected because it would add governance complexity (public criteria, appeals path, ongoing committee workload, politicization risk) without clearly outperforming a more principled approach. Players whose actual level exceeds their domestic ranking — typically because of BWF international experience or because they are competing up an age group — will land in B-flight if their domestic ranking is outside the top 64. This is acknowledged as a real gap and is deferred to a separate workstream rather than solved here.
- **Ranking weights compress modestly** (covered in companion issue): OLC 15% → 20%, CRC dropped from ranking calculation, ORC stays at 50%. This relieves must-attend pressure on ORC by making OLC more rewarding for players who can't crack A-flight at ORC anyway.

How each Context concern is addressed:

| Concern | Proposal addresses by |
|---|---|
| Ranking point incentives drive overcrowding | OLC bumped to 20%; B-flight (= 15% of JN) gives traveled players an OLC-equivalent path; OLC becomes rationally attractive to players unlikely to make A-flight |
| Capping creates fairness problem | B-flight ensures all registrants get a real tournament with meaningful points (B-flight gold equals an A-flight R16 finisher; B-flight beats an A-flight first-round loss). The cap is a clean ranking line — top 64 by national ranking — with no committee-discretion lane that could be politicized. The honest cost is that BWF-experienced and playing-up players whose domestic ranking is outside the top 64 land in B-flight; that residual gap is acknowledged and deferred to a separate workstream. |
| 4-day cost is unacceptable | Format stays Saturday-Monday; no 4th day required |

**Operational impact.** Total matches at the venue are essentially unchanged (128 each at saturation, including 3rd/4th place playoffs). Per-day venue match counts are essentially the same. Two finals on Monday instead of one. Two brackets to publish. The player-welfare improvement comes from one fewer round per player (6 vs 7), which eliminates the forced 3-rounds-in-one-day compression that today's R128/3-day format imposes on deep-run multi-event players.

**Phased rollout proposed.** Pilot the format with one crunch event in one region before expanding nationally.
