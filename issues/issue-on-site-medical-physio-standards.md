## Issue
Major USAB-sanctioned tournaments (JSE, Junior Nationals, Adult Nationals, ORC) do not have guaranteed on-site physiotherapy or medical coverage, despite some regional club-level events (e.g., NorCal OLC) already providing it. USAB lacks a formal medical staffing policy, leaving player safety dependent on individual event organizers with no consistent standard.

**Tags**: `player-safety` `medical` `governance` `junior` `tournaments` `standards`

## Context
Badminton is a high-intensity sport with frequent lower-body, shoulder, and wrist injuries. The risk is elevated at competitive events where players push harder, play multiple matches in a day, and may be away from their regular coaches or medical support.

The current gap is notable on two levels:

1. **Inconsistency across event tiers**: Regional events like ORCs and even NorCal OLC have club-sponsored on-site physio. JSE, a national-level selection event with far higher stakes and athlete volume, does not. This inversion signals the absence of a top-down standard from USAB.

2. **Junior player vulnerability**: JSE and Junior Nationals serve a population of developing athletes. Without on-site physiotherapy, injured juniors may not receive timely assessment, risk aggravating injuries by continuing to play, and have no qualified adult other than a coach making return-to-play calls.

Beyond player welfare, there are organizational risks: without a documented medical staffing policy, USAB and host clubs carry increased liability exposure if a player is injured and no qualified medical personnel were present.

## Potential Options

> The options below are illustrative examples only and are not necessarily the best or final solutions. Better options may come from the community, clubs, or USAB directly.

- **Option A — Mandatory minimum staffing requirements for Tier 1 events**: Define a minimum standard (e.g., one licensed physiotherapist + first aid certified staff) required for all JSE, Junior Nationals, Adult Nationals, and ORC events. Host clubs must meet this standard to be eligible to bid.

- **Option B — Tiered medical coverage by event size and level**: Establish graduated requirements — Tier 1 national events require a licensed physio; Tier 2 regional events require a certified athletic trainer or sports first aider; smaller events require basic first aid. Scales cost and burden proportionally.

- **Option C — USAB-sponsored national medical partner**: USAB contracts with a sports medicine organization or physiotherapy chain as an official sponsor, providing physio staff at sanctioned events at reduced or no cost to host clubs. Sponsorship offsets cost while ensuring credential standards.

- **Option D — University sports medicine partnerships**: Partner with sports medicine or kinesiology programs at local universities to provide supervised student physiotherapists at events. Cost-effective and creates a pipeline of practitioners familiar with badminton-specific injuries.

- **Option E — Cost-sharing model between USAB and host clubs**: USAB subsidizes a portion of medical staffing costs for sanctioned events, with host clubs funding the remainder. Reduces the barrier for smaller clubs to host while maintaining accountability.

## Open Questions

- Does USAB currently have any written policy on medical staffing at sanctioned events? If so, is it enforced?
- Are there BWF or Pan Am Badminton guidelines on medical coverage that USAB should align with?
- What are the insurance and liability implications for USAB and host clubs when no qualified medical staff is present at an event where an injury occurs?
- Who bears the cost — USAB centrally, host clubs as part of their bid obligation, or a shared model?
- What credential level is required — licensed physiotherapist, certified athletic trainer, sports first aider, or EMT?
- Is there currently a formal return-to-play protocol that tournament referees or officials follow when a player is injured on court?
- Have there been specific incidents at past JSE or national events where the absence of medical staff resulted in poor outcomes? Documenting these would strengthen the case for a formal policy.
- Would this requirement affect the pool of clubs willing and able to host national events, and how should that tradeoff be managed?
